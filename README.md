# Composite Autonomous Offerings 

This README accompanies our ICSOC submission. It provides the implementation, experimental evaluation framework, and notebooks needed to replicate the experiments and recreate the figures featured in the paper.

Our approach utilizes Gaussian Processes (GPs) and Deep Gaussian Processes (DGPs) to model individual service capabilities and predict the holistic performance of arbitrary, dynamically-composed service chains under resource-constrained and bottleneck-prone execution environments.

## E1: Experiment #1 — Individual Service Modeling

In E1, we analyze the convergence of the Gaussian Process (GP) that is trained as the agent explores the execution environment randomly. Please create a new venv in Python and install the dependencies in [requirements.txt](requirements.txt).

### 1. Extract Candidates
* **File:** [`1_1_extract_candidates.ipynb`](notebooks/1_1_extract_candidates.ipynb)
* **Description:** We train the GPs with increasing amounts of data (chunked into 20 incremental splits from 5% to 100% of the maximum dataset size) and extract the potential candidate solutions that could be offered.
* **Core Functionality:**
  * Computes and saves the **Log Marginal Likelihood (LML)** across training steps to evaluate how well the GP fits the data (**Fig. 2a**).
  * Uses gradient descent optimization with the `SLSQP` algorithm (restarted 25 times from random positions) to find optimal configurations (**candidates**) under four distinct Service Level Objective (SLO) archetypes: `DEFAULT`, `HIGH_PERF`, `LOW_COST`, and `HIGH_QUALITY`.
  * Incorporates a 5th-percentile penalty scheme to safely down-vote areas in the metric space that carry high prediction uncertainty.
* **Outputs:** Candidate configurations are exported to `statics/candidates/`, and expected SLO fulfillment heatmaps are produced (**Fig. 2b, 2c**). The candidates are then executed in the physical execution environment; we collect the results and follow with the next step.

### 2. Evaluate Candidates
* **File:** [`1_2_evaluate_candidates.ipynb`](notebooks/1_2_evaluate_candidates.ipynb)
* **Description:** We take the metrics collected during physical execution and compare them against the mathematical expectations generated by the isolated agent models.
* **Core Functionality:**
  * Evaluates real-world fitness under the multi-objective utility functions.
  * Measures the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) exclusively for validation runs where the service underperformed relative to the promise (**RMSE for Violations/Shortfalls**).
  * Combines fitness benefits against potential violation penalties ($\beta = 2.5$) to map out a global **Readiness Score**:
    $$\text{readiness} := \text{fitness} - (\text{rmse} \times \beta)$$
* **Outputs:** Plots convergence trends for **Fitness** (**Fig. 3a**), **RMSE for Violations** (**Fig. 3b**), and the **Readiness Score** globally (**Fig. 3c**) as well as isolated per individual SLO subset (**Fig. 4**).

## E2: Experiment #2 — Service Composition & Overhead

This experiment addresses dynamic service compositions during runtime, predicting the performance and multi-tier uncertainty propagation of arbitrary service chains.

### 1. Deep GP Service Composition
* **File:** [`2_create_deepGP_chunk_training.ipynb`](notebooks/2_create_deepGP_chunk_training.ipynb)
* **Description:** Implements, trains, and evaluates Deep Gaussian Processes (DGPs) constructed with `gpytorch` to model concatenated service links.
* **Core Functionality:**
  * Reuses the random-exploration training data from Experiment 1, normalizes the throughput values based on empirical min-max metrics, and directly injects downstream processing bottlenecks (a service cannot surpass the throughput of its predecessor).
  * Structures a `DynamicServiceChain` that maps out deep graph layouts using **Chunk Training** (optimizing layer-specific Variational Evidence Lower Bounds (ELBOs) independently to prevent vanishing/exploding gradients).
  * Uses Monte Carlo sampling (100 samples) to propagate tracking distributions downstream across the pipeline topology.
* **Evaluation Scenarios:** Generates performance profiles and prediction error metrics (**Fig. 5**) for three target pipelines:
  * **Chain 1:** Heterogeneous pipeline ($QR \rightarrow CV \rightarrow PC$)
  * **Chain 2:** Cascaded bottleneck chain ($QR \rightarrow [CV \times 5]$)
  * **Chain 3:** Homogeneous long pipeline ($[QR \times 6]$)

### 2. Computational Overhead Evaluation
* **Description:** Measures runtime execution logs recorded during Experiment 2.1 to evaluate the viability of decentralized decision-making.
* **Observations:** Demonstrates that the processing footprint is heavily dominated by the initial GP training phase (running in milliseconds), whereas graph recomposition and Monte Carlo forward predictions complete in a tiny fraction of that duration (**Table 1**).
